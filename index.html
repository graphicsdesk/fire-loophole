‘The fire loophole’: When tenants are displaced by fires, their landlords can profit

By Amira McKee. Graphics by Kevin Medina and Adina Cazacu-De Luca.

March 23, 2023

Pippa Tsuki Carlson / Senior Staff Photographer

With only 10 minutes to stuff their most important documents into a garbage bag, cousins Yahaira Paulino and Yashira Rodriguez were careful not to drop their flashlight in the dark. Forming soot-covered floors and walls stained “tricolor from the different types of mold,” the five-alarm fire and the torrential water that extinguished it had left their childhood home at 617 W. 141st St. unrecognizable.

Still, they said the extinguishment of the fire on Dec. 3 was just the beginning of a monthslong battle for housing security. Among the 43 families displaced from the building, Paulino’s relatives occupied five of the building’s apartments, leaving few places to turn to after the fire.

617 W. 141st St. is one of 31 West Harlem buildings partially or fully evacuated for fire damage, leaving residents to fend for themselves with little governmental support to ease the yearslong recovery process.

371 out of 719 (51%) of fire evacuations ordered since 2012 have occurred in seven Northern Manhattan neighborhoods, including 54 vacate orders in Hamilton Heights.

Though some residents, including Rodriguez and Paulino, have recently settled into new apartments, most residents remain in homeless shelters or with relatives four months after the blaze at the Hamilton Heights apartment complex, Paulino said in a recent interview with Spectator.

For those displaced from rent-stabilized apartments, the already tumultuous process of finding housing is intensified by the potential “fire loophole,” through which landlords may legally undermine protections for rent-stabilized units under the Housing Stability Tenant Protection Act, intended to close loopholes and bolster tenant protections.

Some tenants’ rights advocates fear that landlords may circumvent the act using renovations or long-haul reconstruction to push rent-stabilized tenants to forfeit their apartments, forcing some long-term residents to remain in homeless shelters for years or risk losing their apartments altogether.

Without formal protections for those displaced by fires, West Harlem resident leaders and tenants’ rights advocates are left to substitute proper social, legal, and economic support to help residents secure long-term housing and eventually return to their homes.

‘Fire victims are multiple victims’

Though the New York City Fire Department and subsequent waves of press coverage reported the five-alarm fire at 617 W. 141st St. as beginning at 1:35 a.m. on Dec. 3, Paulino, who serves as tenant association president for the building, said residents originally reported concerns about smoke to authorities the day prior.

When the tenant of a sixth-floor unit, the eventual nexus of the fire, returned home with his newborn child, he “was greeted with a hazy apartment that smelled like smoke,” she said.

When the fire department arrived in response to the tenant’s call, Paulino said she accompanied the firefighters in investigating the area, even knocking on the doors of neighboring units. Yet, despite the smoke, department officials deemed the building safe and clear of ongoing fire.

“They unplugged stuff and turned off the main breaker, and told him to call the landlord,” Paulino said. “Not even like 20 minutes or 30 minutes later, I had to call them back, like, ‘You got to come back because the smoke in the building is overwhelming.’”

Paulino said the fire was initially discovered by a group of tenants who noticed large quantities of smoke and went to the roof to identify the source.

“[In] maybe like a minute, they opened something up and we just saw all the flames come up,” Paulino said.

Within hours, the upper floors of the building were engulfed in flames, destroying the roof and sixth floor before it was extinguished nearly five hours later, having burned until 6 a.m., according to the fire department. Former department chief John Hodgens said in a Dec. 3 press conference that by the time the department discovered the fire on the sixth floor, it “was already pretty advanced.”

Once the fire was identified, Paulino said evacuating the building’s residents—including the elderly, young children, and individuals with disabilities—in the middle of night was the next major challenge. Rodriguez said she believes that if Paulino had not started calling residents to alert them of the fire, some may not have escaped safely.

“She called me several times telling me to get out,” Rodriguez said. “It was the middle of the night, I was drowsy, people were asleep. We have no alarms that went off, the firefighters did not knock on any doors. They didn’t ... There was no wellness check.”

Rodriguez said that one of her neighbors, an elderly woman, was unable to leave her apartment without assistance. Three hours into the blaze, she was finally rescued.

Yet another tenant of the building didn’t emerge until the next morning, having slept through the night and the ongoing fire, Rodriguez said.

The fire department did not respond to repeated requests for comment.

The fire marshal ruled the fire an accident, caused by a malfunction of electrical wiring in the ceiling of the top floor.

Electrical fires are among the leading causes of structural fires in West Harlem and Upper Manhattan, produced largely by outdated and faulty outlets and wiring, according to the fire department. With age, the wires behind outlets loosen and can eventually break and cause a fire.

617 W 141 St Partners LLC, the owner of the building, faced five unresolved Department of Buildings violations related to building maintenance at the time of the fire, and currently holds another seven violations relating to post-fire evacuation and reconstruction.

617 W 141 St Partners LLC did not respond to repeated requests for comment at the time of publication.

Following a vacate order from the fire marshal, the 37 families living in 617 W. 141st St. were left scrambling to find housing accommodations amid a December blizzard.

Jeremiah Schlotman, managing counsel at the Harlem-based nonprofit People Against Landlord Abuse and Tenant Exploitation, or PA’LANTE, said that regardless of a fire’s origin, the building’s landlord is not obligated to assist in the relocation of the displaced tenants.

“Those vacate orders are what triggers folks’ homelessness,” Schlotman said. “And then, of course, if folks are fortunate enough to have a network that can support them, you’ll see them go in, sometimes into a temporary accommodation with family, friends, things like that. But other than that, folks have to see what they can do […] But if that doesn’t work, then folks are unfortunately limited to the assistance they’ll get from the city.”

Paulino said the city offered displaced families from 617 W. 141st St. one night in a hotel, which could be renewed for an additional two nights by registering with the Department of Housing Preservation and Development. The department’s staff only communicated in English via email, posing a challenge for many of the building’s elderly residents or tenants with limited English skills, Paulino said.

“Our building is mixed with a lot of elderly folks, newer tenants, but mostly the bulk of the building was tenants who lived there for 40, 50 years,” she said. “They raised their family there in stabilized apartments ... and they don’t really speak the language.”

Even after the tenant association successfully registered every resident, displaced families faced a looming eviction date amid a snowstorm, with few resources to find alternative housing.

“PA’LANTE got involved in facilitating that with Shaun Abreu, and he extended it two days so we were able to stay there through the weekend,” Paulino said. “So in total we had seven days in the hotel, but there was this huge snowstorm and that Monday we’re getting kicked out. We call Shawn again and be like ‘What can be done?’ He was like, ‘There’s nothing I can do … you gotta let people be placed in shelters.’”

“Then we’re dealing with a diverse group of people that already have trauma with shelters, who are just crying and really just didn’t know what to do,” she added.

Though she said District 7 city council member Shaun Abreu, CC ’14, was helpful in moving families to compatible shelters, Paulino’s further requests were met with resistance or substitutions. Many residents had lost valuable paperwork needed to register for the housing department or find alternative housing, she said, and local and state politicians including U.S. Rep. Adriano Espaillat helped waive some of the required paperwork for a passport. Tenants were, however, still expected to pay the $300 fee for passports—a heavy burden for many residents who had already experienced significant financial loss.

“You know, there are people that had to pull things together because a lot of people lost their money in the fire because, in true immigrant fashion, they didn’t keep the bulk of their money in the bank. They kept it like under the mattress or somewhere in their apartment,” Rodriguez said.

As part of her lobbying, Paulino asked Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine’s office for support to offset the high costs of relocation for displaced residents.

Levine’s office offered Rockettes tickets.

“People want to be super helpful and say, ‘I donate cans of food.’ But people don’t have stoves to cook the food that you want to donate,” Paulino said. “‘I’ll donate old clothes that I have.’ This is really dope the first two weeks when people don’t have anything, but after that, truly what fire victims need is financial support.”

Without financial support or resources, many displaced residents could not afford the high cost of finding new housing options, exacerbated further by an ongoing citywide housing crisis.

“It was just insane when I’m telling you, like, fire victims are multiple victims.” Paulino said. “They are exploited and victimized a lot, exploited because these [politicians] take a lot of credit, a lot of credit. We just kept seeing the news and all these state officials taking so much credit of helping us because they showed up the night of the fire to say how sorry they were, but there was no follow-through.”

Paulino described the process of seeking help from local officials as a “carousel” in which she was repeatedly redirected without finding the support systems she said city leaders had assured her of the night of the fire.

“If we were any other type of community that didn’t have our zip code, would it be different?” Paulino said. “Because if you’re saying that there are systems in place to help people kind of resettle after a fire, then where was the support? What was the support? It just wasn’t there.”

In the aftermath of the fire, Rodriguez and Paulino have both taken time off from work to lead the tenant association, spending time, energy, and money to help minimize the barriers for other tenants.

“The loophole is really really crazy, and thank God that we had a TA established,” Rodriguez said. “I have no idea how anybody else does it if they don’t have a TA, or a TA that’s capable or willing to help, because the hours that my cousin and I have put into this, the fights with the city, with even management, to let people back in to kind of recover some of their things… Y’all, it’s been crazy.”

‘A lot of games … get played’

Before the fire hit 512 W. 136th St. in 2014, the building was rent-regulated, former resident Tiffany Khan, SIPA ’10, said. Nine years and a renovation later, she said the fire helped her former landlord destabilize the building’s rent—previously as low as $600 for a three-bedroom.

The fire marshal ruled the fire at 512 W. 136th St. an accident caused by “an overloaded power strip under furniture in a first-floor apartment.” The fire killed a 15-year-old resident and injured 12.

A tweet from the fire department the day after the fire commented, “Cause of 8/18 fire in Hamilton Heights (attached image) reminds #NYC about critical fire safety.”

Khan, a Community Board 9 member and board member of the Metropolitan Council on Housing, questioned why the landlord of 512 W. 136th St. did not face greater scrutiny over building maintenance or any obligation to assist in the residents’ relocation.

“The fact that the buildings are not retrofitted to meet 21st century standards or needs is something that the media should be asking,” Khan said.

“Then next thing you know, you have all these politicians and everyone else following suit and saying, ‘Oh, let’s have tenant information sessions or tenant workshops or tenant resources on how to not overload your power strip,’” she added.

To redirect fire-related scrutiny from tenants to landlords, Khan discussed what she calls “fire evictions” and “negligent arson” with “basically anyone who will listen.”

“I’ve been trying to sound the alarm sort of citywide about what I’ve been calling negligent arson and-or fire evictions because it sort of goes to a larger issue,” Khan said at a January CB9 meeting. “You have buildings that have had violations for years, they get ignored, tenants don’t always report them to city agencies. And so what you see that is reported is often the tip of the iceberg. It’s extremely dangerous.”

Much like the residents of 617 W. 141st St., the 100 displaced tenants of 512 W. 136th St. enjoyed a “honeymoon period” in which they were placed in hotels before beginning their stays at shelters.

“As any human being, and I’m pretty sure you and I, if we put ourselves in their shoes, after x number of months or years you would get tired and move on,” Khan said.

Edwin Torres, CB9 health and environment committee co-chair and Hamilton Heights Tenant Association president, said he has seen Hamilton Heights landlords stretch out the time frame for repairs to discourage tenants from waiting to reclaim their rent-stabilized units.

“If you can push [tenants] to look for another place somewhere else, then that means that you’re going to profit from having now another [market price] unit that is not stabilized,” Torres said. “So there are some incentive for these landlords to not move as quickly and expeditiously with the repairs after the fire has taken place.”

In his time as a tenant’s rights lawyer, Schlotman said he has seen reconstructions involve both legitimate roadblocks and landlords “dragging their feet.”

“I often tell my clients to buckle up, to be realistic, plan for maybe even a couple of years,” Schlotman said. “We hope not, we’ll try to keep them honest in the process and use whatever means possible, but that’s kind of what you need to mentally prepare yourself for, because there is a whole process that will legitimately delay the restoration of habitability, but there’s a lot of games that can also get played.”

Between 2007 and 2021, 512 W. 136th St. lost an estimated 14 rent-stabilized units, according to data from the Department of Finance.

Schlotman said he has also seen landlords use “creative” means to circumvent tenant protections outlined in the housing protection act. He described one tactic as “frankensteining,” in which landlords reconfigure apartments to change square footage or combine units to qualify for a new, higher “first rent” from the New York State Division of Housing and Community Renewal.

Still, Khan said about half of the building’s residents remained to fight to move back into their stabilized units.

“Thank God that they did, because I’ve seen other buildings–especially smaller buildings where it’s easier to divide and conquer people—I’ve seen those buildings become entirely free market, lost,” Khan said.

Gabriel, who spoke to Spectator on the condition of anonymity citing concern for retaliation from his landlord and is identified by a pseudonym, was one of the handful of residents who fought to keep their rent-stabilized apartments at 512 W. 136th St., living in a shelter for more than two years while the building was restored. He paid $1 every month in nominal rent to protect his right to return to the unit once it was habitable, Khan said.

Though he said the landlord allowed some tenants to return, he chose to postpone moving in until the restoration was complete, concerned about the impact of construction-related conditions on his immunocompromised child.

Upon returning to the apartment, Gabriel said the landlord told him that he owed back-rent for the years he lived in a shelter and withheld belongings that had survived the fire. Gabriel, who now lives in the Bronx, vacated the apartment, which was converted to market rate upon his forfeit, Khan said.

512-514 Realty LLC, the owner of 512 W. 136th St., did not respond to repeated requests for comment.

In 2015, Mark Lovinger, the building manager at 512 W. 136th St., denied deliberately waiting for apartments to be destabilized, telling the former online news organization DNAinfo, "I don’t see why they think they are at risk."

‘Fired out’

At a January CB9 housing, land use, and zoning meeting, PA’LANTE founder and executive director Elsia Vasquez spoke to the demand for legal services for displaced tenants in West Harlem. She said the nonprofit’s small legal team was actively representing fire victims from three different buildings—617 W. 141st St., 695 Saint Nicholas St., and 111 W. 135th St. In total, Vasquez said, PA’LANTE has represented 331 individuals in the last year and held 186 client meetings between July and December 2022.

“These cases are not straight cookie cutter cases, they’re not 12-month period cases, these cases are long term. We’re looking at a minimum of three to four years,” Vasquez said at the meeting. “We know we’re going to have to aggressively fight and litigate in court because landlords right now are going to try to use these catastrophes that happen in our community as an opportunity to make money.”

Schlotman said for many tenants of West Harlem and Upper Manhattan, legal representation is critical to help prevent landlord abuse and exploitation.

“Particularly when you’re talking about uptown Manhattan, you’re talking about areas with a lot of displacement and gentrification … pushing out a lot of long-term residents, many of whom are working class or lower income, but have been here for decades,” Schlotman said. “So you’re seeing a lot of pressure. Some of it is legal, and a lot of it’s not. But regardless of whether it’s legal or illegal, having more attorneys, representation, and eyes on the tenant side of things can go a long way toward combating the deleterious effects of displacement.”

Torres, who also serves as a PA’LANTE board member, said providing immediate legal representation for fire victims is essential. For the residents of 617 W. 141st St., Torres said PA’LANTE had legal advisors prepared to step in within two days of the fire.

“When it happens, the Red Cross comes in and provides the first aid, but after the honeymoon passes, that’s when the headaches begin and that is the legal process of trying to get back into your home,” Torres said. “We have been helping the community tremendously, but [PA’LANTE lawyers] are stretched to the limit. There’s such a demand for providing representations to the tenants that they’re doing what they can. We need more resources.”

As West Harlem resident leaders step up alongside community-based organizations to support local fire victims, Rodriguez and Paulino reflected on the sacrifices they’ve made to protect their neighbors and themselves. From collating the locations and housing status of each resident to bridging gaps in language barriers and serving as emotional support, Paulino said, “I get, like, tingles up my spine every time that I have to talk about what December was for us.”

“It took us a while to finally be in a space that we weren’t fearful every time we heard firefighters, and people still are,” Paulino said. “There’s folks that are still in meetings telling us that they’re very depressed and that the conditions in the shelters are not the best. They have no one to speak to, so imagine having all of this on top of your shoulders. You know, it’s more than we ever thought we’d be able to handle, and we’ll do best as we can, but it’s a lot.”

Still, Paulino and Rodriguez have continued working to organize and support their tenant association, holding up to three-hour monthly meetings in both English and Spanish.

Despite the long road ahead, Rodriguez and Paulino said they remain optimistic about returning to their childhood home. While they’ve adopted a tentative two-year timeline, they said they hope their fellow tenants find comfort as they wait for 617 W. 141st St. to be renovated. Still, Paulino said the tenants of her building, much like any fire-displaced community, are fatigued by insecure housing and inconsistent resources months after the traumatic night.

“This is something that people are either going to have to muscle through, or find other family members to help them—be at the mercy of people. Which, let me be real with you, it gets old,” Paulino said. “People are already tired of like, ‘Oh my god, it’s March. What do you mean you don’t have an apartment?’ We used to get that all the time. And we were like, ‘You have no idea how hard it is.’”
